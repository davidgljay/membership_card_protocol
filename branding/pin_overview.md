# Pin Protocol
*A tool for building building trust and exchanging resources in community.*

## What’s a pin?
A pin is a digital id that communities can give out to prove that people are trusted. It can be used to access information, conversations, and resources anywhere on the internet. If you choose to show it, it offers strong proof that you have status in a community. If you choose not to show it, no one knows that it exists.

## Pins make it easy to create community resources
Taryn decides to try creating a pin and giving it out to her neighbors. She sends them a link or has them scan a QR code at the local block party, and they do the facial recognition checkmark thing on their phones to accept it. Now it’s easy for anyone to create a place anywhere on the internet that’s only accessible to people from the neighborhood.

One neighbor decides to create neighbors-only list of free stuff. Another creates a community calendar that requires a neighborhood pin to view and post. A kid decides to create a neighbors-only Minecraft server. When someone loads up the server they just need to drag their pin from a wallet to the login screen to enter.


## Pins help you know who to trust
Taryn is also an OBGYN. Recently there has been a lot of misinformation online attacking potentially life-saving procedures in labor and delivery. Taryn wants to push back, but she’s afraid of flack coming down on her or the hospital where she works. She makes a new, anonymous account and attaches a pin to it proving that she’s an OBGYN, then begins sharing information about why this procedure is necessary when birth complications arise.

Sure enough her replies and DMs begin to get flooded, but when she sorts for people who are verified doctors and midwives she finds a lot of gratitude and agreement, which is reassuring. She ignores the flood of DMs but one catches her eye- it’s attached to a pin proving that the sender is a reporter from a major newspaper. She responds, and agrees to provide anonymous quotes for a story on the procedure.

## Pins enable accountability for bad behavior
Taryn decides to ignore most of the hate coming her way, but a friend agrees to look through for any that are especially worrying. Thankfully because Taryn’s pin can’t be traced back to her there’s little that the mob can do, but there are a few things that seem like pretty disturbing threats. One comes from a woman who seems like she needs help and has attached a pin for her church and her local PTA. 

After some consideration, Taryn clicks a button that reports what the woman said to her church. While Taryn doesn’t know who this woman is the church that gave out her pin does, and they’ve just received strongly verified proof of her behavior. Taryn hopes that the woman’s pastor will know how to step in and help her calm down.


## Pins are private and secure
The pins that Taryn is using are easy to set up and easy to use, but are backed by strong cryptography. No one but Taryn knows what pins she has, not even the tech companies hosting the infrastructure that make pins possible. No one knows how Taryn is using her pins but Taryn, unless she chooses to share that information or is reported to a pin granter.

Sharing a pin also lays a foundation for encrypted communication that is resistant to both man-in-the-middle attacks and decryption by quantum computers. Pins make strong privacy and security a default wherever they are used.

## Pins are owned by the people and organizations that use them
The Pin Protocol is decentralized. Taryn can freely switch which software she uses to store her pins or create new ones. When deciding on a new piece of software, she can examine reviews from experts in security and trust and safety to ensure that it is well designed and thoroughly tested. If a software program tries to do something sneaky, like pushing an update that spies on the pins of its users, it will quickly be discovered by experts reviewing its codebase and its reputation will crash.


## With consent, pins are a powerful way to collect data about communities
Taryn regularly uses her OBGYN pin to hang out with other doctors both online and offline. Through it she’s discovered a local monthly birth worker karaoke night as well as a private forum where professionals break down the latest medical research. One day she gets a message from ACOG, the national association of OBGYNs, asking if she’ll donate some date. She wouldn’t have time for a traditional survey, but she clicks yes, and sends over anonymized data about where and how frequently she’s using her pin.

ACOG now has a rich, anonymized picture of where and how OBGYNs are connecting across the internet, giving their staff insight about what their members want and where to show up to support them. It also tells them who in their community is most active so that they can pull those people into conversation. After their big annual conference they are pleased to see a notable uptick in pin metrics, and proudly show their funders that their goal of building richer connections in the field is being met.
